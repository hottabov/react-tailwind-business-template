You know that feeling when a tradie says, “Yeah mate, no worries, we’ll sort it,” and somehow that sentence gives you both comfort and mild financial anxiety at the same time? Hiring a painter can feel exactly like that. The quote looks fine. The photos on their website look polished. They sound confident on the phone. Then two weeks later you are staring at streaky walls, paint on the floor, and an invoice that somehow grew extra limbs.

That is the problem with hiring painters. A good one can lift the entire house, protect your surfaces, and make the place feel sharper, cleaner, and more valuable. A bad one can leave you with peeling paint, rushed prep, awkward excuses, and the kind of “warranty” that disappears the moment you try to use it.

So before you hire anyone, ask better questions.

Not vague ones. Not polite filler. Real questions that expose whether you are dealing with a professional or someone who mainly specialises in sounding available.

If you are looking for a **house painter in Melbourne**, these are the ten questions worth asking before the first drop sheet hits the floor.

## Why Choosing the Right Painter Matters

Painting looks deceptively simple from the outside. Most homeowners see colour going on the wall and assume that is the job.

It is not.

A quality paint job depends on:

- surface preparation  
- product choice  
- proper repairs  
- correct primers  
- clean cutting-in  
- consistent finish  
- protection of floors, furniture, and fixtures  
- realistic timelines  
- clear communication  

That is why two painters can quote the “same job” and deliver completely different results.

One quote might include proper filling, sanding, gap sealing, stain blocking, premium products, and two full coats.

The cheaper quote might include hope.

This matters whether you are repainting a weatherboard in Preston, a unit in Moonee Ponds, a family home in Glen Waverley, or a townhouse in Brunswick. The postcode changes. The risk stays the same.

## 1. Are You Licensed and Insured?

This is the first question because it removes a lot of nonsense quickly.

Any painter doing significant domestic painting work should be able to explain their licensing position and show **public liability insurance**. If they dodge the question, get vague, or act offended that you asked, that is not professionalism. That is a warning.

You want to know:

- are they properly registered or licensed for the work they do  
- do they have current public liability insurance  
- do they have coverage for property damage or on-site issues  
- are they operating as a legitimate business  

A real business does not panic when asked for basic proof.

## 2. What Preparation Is Included in the Quote?

This is one of the most important questions in the whole process.

Cheap quotes often stay cheap by cutting the prep.

That means less:

- sanding  
- filling  
- gap sealing  
- patch repair  
- stain treatment  
- scraping of loose paint  
- priming where needed  

And prep is where the durability of the whole job lives.

So ask for specifics.

Not “we do all prep.”

Ask exactly what is included. For example:

- Are holes and cracks being filled?  
- Is sanding included?  
- Are glossy surfaces being deglossed or sanded?  
- Will peeling paint be scraped back properly?  
- Are water stains being sealed with the correct primer?  
- Is caulking included around trims and gaps?  

If the answer is vague, the finish probably will be too.

## 3. What Paint Brands and Products Do You Use?

This is not about being snobbish about brands. It is about understanding standards.

Professional painters usually work with well-known systems because they know how the products behave, what finish they produce, and how they hold up over time. They should be able to tell you what they plan to use on:

- walls  
- ceilings  
- trims  
- doors  
- exterior surfaces  
- problem areas needing primer  

A good answer sounds specific.

A weak answer sounds like:
“Whatever’s on special.”
That is not a system. That is a clearance aisle strategy.

You want to know:

- which brand they use  
- which line of product they use  
- why they chose it for your job  
- whether primer is included where required  

The best painters do not just mention the brand. They explain the reasoning.

## 4. How Many Coats Are Included?

You would think this would be obvious. It is not.

Some homeowners assume “painting the room” means proper coverage with enough coats to look right. Some painters quote in a way that leaves room for interpretation. That is how arguments happen.

Ask clearly:

- how many coats are included on walls  
- how many on ceilings  
- how many on trims and doors  
- whether spot priming or full priming is included  
- what happens if coverage is poor because of a colour change  

This matters a lot when going:

- from dark to light  
- from light to dark  
- over repaired patches  
- over stained surfaces  
- over porous or previously uneven finishes  

A quote without clear coat expectations is a quote designed to become slippery later.

## 5. What Exactly Is Excluded From the Quote?

This question is underrated and brutally effective.

Most people ask what is included. Fewer ask what is not. That is where hidden costs tend to live.

Ask what the quote excludes, including things like:

- major plaster repairs  
- wallpaper removal  
- mould treatment  
- moving heavy furniture  
- doors or trims not listed  
- exterior timber replacement  
- access equipment  
- parking or permit costs  
- additional coats due to colour changes  

A solid painter will usually answer this clearly, because they have thought it through already.

A shaky one often has not.

And that is the point. You are not just comparing prices. You are comparing clarity.

## 6. Do You Offer a Written Workmanship Warranty?

A verbal promise means very little once the invoice is paid and the ute is gone.

If a painter offers a warranty, ask for it **in writing**. You want to know:

- what is covered  
- how long it lasts  
- what is excluded  
- whether product and workmanship issues are treated differently  
- what the process is if something fails  

A proper painter should be able to explain this without becoming theatrical.

No warranty at all does not automatically mean they are bad. But vague “don’t worry, we stand by our work” language is weak. A real business should be able to put that confidence into writing.

## 7. Who Will Actually Be Doing the Work?

This question matters more than homeowners realise.

Sometimes the person quoting the job is also the painter doing the work. Great. Sometimes they run a team. Also fine. Sometimes they outsource large chunks of the work and the actual crew is a mystery until day one.

You need to know:

- who will be on site  
- whether employees or subcontractors are being used  
- who supervises the work  
- whether the quoted standard is the standard the crew actually delivers  

Because the quality of the quote meeting means very little if the real job gets handed to whoever happened to be free that week.

## 8. How Long Will the Job Take?

You are not looking for a fantasy answer here. You are looking for an honest one.

Ask:

- when they can start  
- how long the job will take  
- whether the work is consecutive or split around other projects  
- what could affect the timeline  
- how they handle weather delays for exterior work  

A good painter will give you a reasonable timeframe, not just what sounds pleasing.

Be careful with anyone who promises suspiciously fast turnaround on a job that clearly needs prep. Fast can be efficient. It can also mean shortcuts.

## 9. How Will You Protect My Home During the Job?

A decent painter is not just judged by the final walls. They are judged by how they treat the property while they are there.

Ask how they protect:

- floors  
- furniture  
- benchtops  
- windows  
- fittings  
- gardens and paths on exterior jobs  

Also ask whether they:

- remove switch plates  
- use proper drop sheets  
- mask carefully  
- clean up daily  
- dispose of waste properly  

Some painters leave a site looking almost untouched except for the improved paintwork. Others leave behind tape scraps, dust, drips, and a strong sense that standards were optional.

Guess which version you want.

## 10. Can You Show Recent Local Work or Reviews?

Anyone can post a few polished photos online. What you want is evidence that they are doing solid work for real clients now.

Ask for:

- recent reviews  
- photos of completed work  
- examples of similar projects  
- jobs completed in Melbourne or nearby suburbs  
- references if appropriate for larger projects  

This is especially useful if your home type matches the work they often do. A painter who regularly handles weatherboards in Essendon or family homes in Bentleigh may be a better fit than someone whose portfolio is mostly new-build interiors.

Look for consistency, not just one perfect highlight reel.

## Red Flags to Watch For

Some warning signs are obvious. Others are dressed up as confidence.

Watch out for:

- vague quotes  
- no clear prep details  
- refusal to provide insurance details  
- pressure to commit quickly  
- extremely low pricing compared with everyone else  
- unclear product answers  
- no written warranty  
- poor communication before the job even begins  
- inconsistent reviews or strange gaps in proof of work  

A painter does not need to sound polished like a corporate sales rep. But they do need to sound clear, structured, and accountable.

## A Real-Life Example From a Melbourne Homeowner

A homeowner in Melbourne’s north-west received three quotes for an interior repaint. One was the cheapest by a decent margin. On paper, it looked like a win.

Then they started asking questions.

What prep was included? Vague answer.
How many coats? “Usually two, depending.”
What warranty? “We always look after our customers.”
What products? “Good stuff.”
Who would be doing the work? “One of the boys.”

That quote was not cheaper because the painter had discovered a secret way to deliver premium work at a discount. It was cheaper because the detail was missing.

The homeowner chose a more expensive quote from a painter who explained the prep, listed the products, defined the exclusions, and gave a written workmanship commitment.

The result cost more upfront and far less in stress.

That is usually how this works.

## Final Thoughts

So, what should you ask before hiring a **painter in Melbourne**?

Ask whether they are licensed and insured. Ask what prep is included. Ask what products they use. Ask how many coats are included. Ask what is excluded. Ask about warranty, timelines, site protection, who will do the work, and whether they can prove recent results.

That is how you separate a professional from a gamble.

And that tradie line from the beginning — “Yeah mate, no worries, we’ll sort it”? Sometimes it comes from someone excellent. Sometimes it comes from someone who has built a whole business model around relaxed language and low accountability.

Your job is not to be impressed by confidence. Your job is to ask enough questions that confidence has to become evidence.

Do that, and your odds improve fast.

## FAQ: Hiring a Painter in Melbourne

### What should I ask a painter before hiring them?
Ask about licensing, insurance, preparation, products, number of coats, exclusions, warranty, timeline, site protection, and recent work.

### Why is preparation so important in a painting quote?
Because preparation is where cheap quotes often cut corners. Poor prep leads to peeling, patchy finish, visible defects, and shorter paint life.

### Should a painter provide a written warranty?
Yes, ideally. A written workmanship warranty is far more useful than a verbal promise and gives you something clear to rely on if issues come up.

### Is the cheapest painting quote usually the best?
Usually not. A much cheaper quote often means reduced prep, lower-grade materials, unclear scope, or omitted work that becomes an extra later.

### How do I know if a painter is professional?
A professional painter gives clear answers, explains the scope properly, uses recognised products, provides proof of insurance, communicates well, and does not rely on vague promises.
