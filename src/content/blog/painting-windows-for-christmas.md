As November rolls around in Melbourne, the weather warms up, the days stretch out, and the countdown to Christmas begins. It’s the time of year when we throw open the windows to let the breeze in and start preparing our homes for family gatherings and festive parties. 

If you want to give your home a noticeable lift before the guests arrive—without spending a fortune or committing to a massive renovation—tackling your window frames and sills is the perfect weekend project.

## Why Focus on Windows?

Windows naturally draw the eye. They frame your view of the garden and let in the beautiful summer light. Over the year, window sills gather dust, water marks from condensation, and minor scratches from everyday life. A fresh coat of crisp white paint instantly makes a room feel cleaner and more loved.

## The Minimal-Effort Preparation

You don’t need to strip the timber back to bare wood unless the old paint is badly peeling. For a quick cosmetic refresh:
- **Clean thoroughly:** Sugar soap is your best friend. Wipe away all the grime and greasy fingerprints.
- **Light sand:** Use a fine-grit sandpaper (around 120-grit) just to take the shine off the old paint and give the new coat something to grip.
- **Dust and tape:** Wipe away the dust with a damp cloth and use good quality painter's tape along the glass to ensure sharp, professional lines.

## Choosing the Right Paint

For interior trims like windows and sills, an acrylic water-based enamel (like Dulux Aquanamel or Taubmans Water Based Enamel) is ideal. It dries quickly, doesn't have the strong lingering smell of traditional oil paints, and won't yellow over time. 

A semi-gloss or gloss finish is best, as it reflects the twinkling lights of the Christmas tree and is easy to wipe clean when the summer dust blows in.

## A Festive Finishing Touch

Once the paint is dry and the tape is peeled away, your windows will look brand new. It’s the perfect clean canvas to hang a simple pine wreath, string up some fairy lights, or display your favourite festive candles along the freshly painted sill.
